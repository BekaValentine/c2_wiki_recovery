Six Thinking Hats

Six Thinking Hats by EdwardDeBono

[ISBN 03-1617-831-4] paperback [ISBN 03-1617-791-1] hardcover (out of print)

The book describes a system for managing discussion where reports are classified under six headings, each getting a hat of characteristic color. These are:

  • WhiteHat : JustTheFacts, unemotional data and questions about getting more data.
  • BlackHat : TheDownSide, negative consequences, the worst things that could happen.
  • YellowHat: TheUpSide, positive consequences, the best things that could happen.
  • GreenHat : InnovativeIdeas, off-the-wall creativity.
  • RedHat : GutFeelings, emotions, what you really think in your heart and belly.
  • BlueHat : MetaThinking, process and procedure.

Whether you are running a meeting or you are alone and want to ensure that you are considering everything relevant, what you do is set up a sequence of hats, such as blue -- red -- yellow -- black -- white -- green -- red -- blue, and then (get everyone to) put on each hat in the sequence and try to produce thoughts of that kind. In a meeting you invite all the participants to put on the same hat at the same time. If two contradictory thoughts pop up under the same hat, that's fine: both of them are put down in parallel and the contradiction is resolved later. At the end of the sequence you should have a balanced set of information that you can base a decision on.

Some things the six hats are not:

  • They are not classifications for thoughts which have already occurred. Rather, they are directions in which you should intend to think. You should set out to produce thoughts that will go under a specific hat; you put on your WhiteHat and produce some factual thoughts, then you put on your RedHat and produce some emotional thoughts, and so forth. You do not do some random thinking and then say, "Ah, I see that that was YellowHat thinking." (This is why using the hats as Wiki tags or WikiBadges, on already-written information, is a bad idea. A good idea, though, might be SixThinkingHatsOnaWikiPage.)
  • They are not classifications for people. While it may be that some people prefer certain kinds of hats, it is not a good idea to attach that person to that hat. The whole value of the SixThinkingHats is that each person learns to think with all of them (one at a time) and thus gets a more balanced view.
  • They are not "dramatic identities" or characters in a play. In a SixThinkingHats discussion, everybody wears the same hat at the same time. For example, everybody may start with the white hat, then everybody will switch to the red hat, then everybody will switch to the blue hat. It is not correct to assign one person to be the blue hat, and another to be the red hat, and set up a debate. The whole point of the six thinking hats is to allow a discussion where all points of view are considered -- while debate is eliminated entirely! (In a debate, the participants have a tendency to entrench their own positions rather than exploring the opponent's point of view.) This also implies that, in SixThinkingHatsOnaWikiPage, each hat will have its own section on the page, and people will add in their thoughts under that hat -- rather than, e.g., the mess on WhyWeHateLisp.

What the hat metaphor does:

  • It better separates fact from opinion,
  • It more easily identifies important parts of the decision making process,
  • It provides a neutral language for meta-discussion. You can say, "Take off your BlackHat for a moment and look at the upside." The idea is not that BlackHat is wrong, but that it is not the appropriate hat here. All the hats are equally valuable.
  • It prevents arguments by keeping everybody under the same hat at the same time, and putting down contradictory ideas in parallel, and
  • It provides a discipline to ensure that the issue under consideration is seen from many sides (e.g., from the points of view of all six hats).

There are many occasions where wearing a black hat can be useful. When an engineer in Chernobyl said "let's see if we can keep the reactor cooling working by the inertia of the generator during a shutdown", someone with a black hat would have had a very useful contribution to make....

This seems double-edged to me. Clearly the idea is that talking about hats encourages people to disengage themselves from their arguments ("this isn't me speaking, it's my BlackHat") and that this is a Good Thing. But isn't it also likely to be a tool for devaluing and trivialising people's opinions or feelings ("what you're saying is just your BlackHat talking, and we don't need to take it seriously because we can just ask you to TakeOffTheHat instead") ? This SixHats business seems to deny the fact that some opinions are, rightly, more than hat-deep.

Hats in DecisionMaking

The SixThinkingHats seem to be primarily useful for making decisions. Thus, a certain way to proceed is called for.

  • InitialBlueHat
  • For each apparent option, RedHat WhiteHat YellowHat BlackHat.
  • GreenHat to try to see if there are any more options
  • RedHat about which option might be the best.
  • FinalBlueHat.

Sometimes there is apparently only one option, or two (do it or don't do it). In that case, the plan is much simpler.

Metaphors and Concepts

I think there is a conceptual error in some of the assumptions discussed here. The SixThinkingHats metaphor is used to get all the dimensions of a participant's mind and thinking. Human beings tend to have more than one face, but they expose only one at a given moment. The SixThinkingHats system tries to expose the other faces by disconnecting the person from the comment, allowing more detailed input on some discussion. Ridiculing someone's 'HatThoughts' is not a bad practice, but is instead a sign of the immaturity of the offender and should be eliminated from any discussions. Discussions, workshops, and reviews are meant to improve and help to address the needs of the team, not to find ScapeGoats. -- EnriqueCombaRiepenhausen
