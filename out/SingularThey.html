In contexts like "everyone should give their variables meaningful names" or "has anyone lost their pencil", "they" has been used by native speakers of English for centuries. To me, at least, it feels entirely natural in such sentences - more so than any other pronoun would. There are other contexts, usually when the gender of the pronoun's antecedent is not clear, where "they" does grate the ear, but does so less than alternatives such as "he" or "he or she".

You could say "everyone should give meaningful names to variables" and "has anyone lost a pencil", the possessive is extra fluff in these cases.

This is not to say that "they" can be used strictly as a singular pronoun (though it seems quite possible that it will be in a few centuries), but apparently singular uses of it do seem to be standard English in some cases. In any case, it seems like an oversimplification to treat SingularThey as nothing more than a well-intentioned but incorrect innovation in usage. People do use SingularThey to avoid gender specific language, but when they do, they are generalizing from uses of "they" that were already accepted.

Not Singular, Not Plural

The difficulty is in finding an appropriate pronoun for "one." "One" is a concept and does not have either a singular or plural. It is similar to "water," so the pronoun "it" might seem appropriate, except that "it" also has a connotation of being less than human. Thus the most grammatically correct option is rejected out of hand. This leaves the following alternatives.

  • He - This probably has the most historical basis and is usually unnoticed by readers.
  • S/He - This is an admirable attempt to create a new pronoun, except that it only works in written text.
  • Alternate He and She (usually at a paragraph by paragraph level) - This also is usually unnoticed by readers, but requires a little more effort by authors. It can be time consuming to maintain when adding, deleting, or inserting paragraphs. It is also very difficult to keep track of in oral speech.
  • They - This is usually unnoticed in oral speech but is found to be jarring in written text by some readers in certain contexts. In other contexts, it is unnoticed. For example, "They are spying on me." "Someone is spying on you?" would probably not raise objections.
  • You - This has the advantage of being non-specific in terms of plurality versus singularity. Use of "you" as an indefinite third person pronoun is quite common in written text and oral speech. It has the disadvantage, though, of also being the second person pronoun and can cause a general statement to be interpreted as a personal attack. For example, compare "One should strive to use proper grammar." with "You should strive to use proper grammar." Of the various alternatives, this is the only one that I would strongly advise speakers and writers to avoid.
  • We - This is sometimes used as an alternative to "you." The intent is to dull the personal implications of a statement by including the author as well. This can backfire, however, if the audience rejects the author as a member of the group.
